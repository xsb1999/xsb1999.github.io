「EmotionalChatbot」 Chatbot With Emotional Context

Posted on 2022-06-14 Edited on 2022-06-20 Views: 5.1k 5 mins.

Introduction

At present, chatbots have two main drawbacks. One is the lack of distinct personality, that is, different chatbots give basically the same response to a user’s same input sentence, which lacks the uniqueness of each chatbot. The other drawback is the poor emotional retention ability of chatbots, that is, they do not take into account the emotional context, which makes the chatbot’s mood change dramatically in an instant during the conversation, lacking realism. Therefore, it is of great significance to research, design and implement chatbots with multi-personality, good ability of maintaining emotions and possessing emotional context.

In this project, I designed and implemented 5 chatbots with different personalities (Angry, Sad, Happy, Neutral, Frustrated) and strong emotional retention ability. I also effectively improved the ability of these chatbots to maintain emotions, reducing the probability of chatbots’ mood changes, making their conversations with people more realistic and vivid.

A Small Demo

1. Choose a chatbot

Let’s choose Susan, whose personality is Sad.

2. Chat with it

First, I say "i am so happy", my emotion is happy, but Susan responds with sadness, with the sentence "i am not". The reason why the chatbot’s emotion is sad is that Susan’s personality is sad, so that she has the ability to maintain it’s personality, the same as other chatbots.

Then, I say "i am excited", my emotion is still happy. This time, Susan responds with happiness, with the sentence "yeah yeah yeah yeah". This is like talking with a real person. When I keep saying happy words to a person, I will change its mood. The accumulation of emotions causes changes. When I repeatedly said happy words to the chatbot, happy emotions built up, causing the chatbot’s mood to change from sad to happy.

After that, I say "it's good" and "it's wonderful", which are both happy sentences. The chatbot’s emotion keeps to be happy with no changes.

Then, I say "i feel worse" which is a sad sentence, and my mood is sad. The chatbot’s mood remains happy, instead of turning into sadness. This is because the chatbot has such a large accumulation of happy emotions that only one sad sentence is not enough to turn its mood directly into sadness. This is the emotional retention ability of chatbots.

Finally, I say "hello sir", a sentence with neutral emotion, and the chatbot responds with sadness, with the sentence "i do not want to talk anymore". That is because I designed a emotional recovery mechanism for chatbots. After a certain number of rounds of conversation, the chatbot’s emotions moved toward its personality. It is worth noting that this movement of emotion does not directly turn emotion into personality, but gradually move closer to personality.

Background

Chatbots are booming and have entered people’s lives. Chatbots can be used as NPC in games and can also be used for Chit-chat to accompany people.

Current chatbots have two drawbacks

  • Not having unique personalities
    • All brands of chatbots gave roughly the same response to the same user input.
  • Poor emotional retention ability

Overall Project Structure

The project contains 3 core functional modules

  • Emotion Recognition Module
  • Emotion Response Module
  • Dialogue Generation Module

Emotion Recognition Module

  • Input: User input sentence
  • Output: User emotion

  • Dataset
    • IEMOCAP
    • 151 dialogues, 7433 sentences
  • Data preprocessing stage
    • Merge data from the Excited class into the Happy class.
    • Extract dialogue text and corresponding emotion labels from multiple txt files.
    • Save the data to a pkl file.
  • Vocabulary construction stage
    • Extract all words from the conversation dataset.
    • Add four additional words, namely, <SOS>, <EOS>, <UNK>, <PAD>.
    • Map each word to a unique numeric id, and saved as word2id and id2word.
    • With a total of 3125 words in the final vocabulary.

  • Modeling stage

    • Use Tensorflow’s Keras framework.
    • Use double - layer two-way LSTM (shown in the figure below).
    • Model training

  • Model evaluation

    • training set
    • testing set
  • Functional test

Emotion Response Module

  • Input: User emotion
  • Output: Chatbot response emotion

  • Emotional models of chatbots

    • An emotion vector E is defined to represent the emotion of chatbot.
    • e1, e2, e3, e4 and e5 correspond to angry, sad, happy, neutral and frustrated respectively.
    • An emotional sensitivity vector ES was defined to represent five chatbots with different personalities.
    • S1, S2, S3, S4, and S5 correspond to angry, sad, happy, neutral, and frustrated respectively.
    • Mapping between charters and emotional sensitivity vectors of chatbot.

  • Emotional response algorithm for chatbot

    • Accumulation of emotion
      • During a chat, the chatbot accumulates emotions and “remembers” historical conversations.
    • Emotional recovery
      • After every 5 rounds of conversation, the chatbot’s emotion returned in the direction of its personality.
    • Flow chat of the algorithm

Dialogue Generation Module

  • Input: Chatbot response emotion & User input sentence
  • Output: Chatbot response sentence

  • Modeling stage

    • Use Tensorflow’s Keras framework.
    • Use Seq2Seq model.
